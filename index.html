Used SUV Analysis: Kia EV9 vs Toyota Highlander Hybrid

Financial perspective on optimal purchase timing, mileage, and options

Key Requirements

  • Budget: <$25,000
  • Third‑row seating required
  • Max mileage at purchase: <75,000 miles
  • Preferred: Certified Pre‑Owned (CPO)
  • Location: Southern California (willing to travel)
  • Annual mileage: ~10,000 miles; ownership horizon: 5‑10 years

Market Overview (as of mid‑2026)

Kia EV9

The 2024 Kia EV9 has seen steep depreciation, losing about 49% of its value (~$27,900) in roughly 2‑3 years, with a current resale value around $28,500 (trade‑in ~$26,600). Early 2025 models retain ~75‑80% of their original MSRP after one year.

Because the EV9 is a relatively new all‑electric SUV (launched 2023), the used market is still thin, and prices for sub‑$25k examples with <75k miles are extremely scarce in Southern California. Most listings are well above $30k, even for higher‑mileage units.

Toyota Highlander Hybrid

Toyota’s hybrid SUV holds its value far better. A 2020 Highlander Hybrid LE starts at about $25,100 (fair purchase) in good condition, and prices rise quickly with trim and mileage. Finding a sub‑$25k Highlander Hybrid with <75k miles is challenging; most viable options are older (2015‑2019) or have higher mileage (>90k).

Depreciation is modest: the 2025 model is projected to lose $21,920 over five years, leaving a ~5‑year residual value of $26,350 (KBB).

Depreciation Comparison

Interpretation: The EV9 loses value quickly in the first three years (front‑loaded depreciation typical of EVs), while the Highlander Hybrid depreciates more linearly and retains a higher percentage of its original price over the same period.

Best Time to Buy (Financial Perspective)

Kia EV9

Given the steep early‑year drop, the optimal window for a used EV9 under $25k is likely late 2026 to early 2027, when 2023‑2024 models reach 2‑3 years of age and mileage approaches 30k‑50k. However, even then, finding one under $25k with <75k miles in Southern California may require expanding the search nationally or considering higher mileage (>75k) or non‑CPO units.

Toyota Highlander Hybrid

Because the Highlander Hybrid depreciates slowly, the best financial sense lies in purchasing a slightly older model (2018‑2020) with moderate mileage (60k‑80k) that has already absorbed the bulk of early depreciation. For a sub‑$25k target, aim for late 2026 onward**, when 2018‑2020 models fall into the price range. Purchasing a CPO example adds a premium but can reduce risk.

Recommendation

Under the strict constraints (<$25k, <75k miles, CPO preferred), the Toyota Highlander Hybrid is the more realistic choice. Look for:

  • Model years: 2018‑2020
  • Trim: LE or XLE (base trims give best value)
  • Mileage: 60k‑80k (still plenty of life left for 5‑10 years at 10k mi/yr)
  • Purchase window: Q3 2026 – Q2 2027 (when prices dip as newer 2021‑2023 models enter the used market)
  • Consider expanding search to nearby states (Arizona, Nevada) if Southern California inventory is limited.

If you can flexibility on price (<$30k) or mileage (<=90k), a 2023‑2024 Kia EV9 becomes viable and offers lower operating costs (electricity vs. gasoline) and potential tax incentives for used EVs.
