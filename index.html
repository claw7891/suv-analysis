Used SUV Analysis: Kia EV9 vs Toyota Highlander Hybrid

Financial perspective on optimal purchase timing, mileage, and total cost of ownership

  • Annual mileage: ~10,000 miles; ownership horizon: 5‑10 years

Kia EV9

The 2024 Kia EV9 has seen steep depreciation, losing about 49% of its value (~$27,900) in roughly 2‑3 years, with a current resale value around $28,500 (trade‑in ~$26,600). Early 2025 models retain ~75‑80% of their original MSRP after one year. Because the EV9 is a relatively new all‑electric SUV (launched 2023), the used market is still thin, and prices for sub‑$25k examples with <75k miles are extremely scarce in Southern California. Most listings are well above $30k, even for higher‑mileage units.

Toyota Highlander Hybrid

Toyota's hybrid SUV holds its value far better. A 2020 Highlander Hybrid LE starts at about $25,100 (fair purchase) in good condition, and prices rise quickly with trim and mileage. Finding a sub‑$25k Highlander Hybrid with <75k miles is challenging; most viable options are older (2015‑2019) or have higher mileage (>90k). Depreciation is modest: the 2025 model is projected to lose $21,920 over five years, leaving a ~5‑year residual value of $26,350 (KBB).

How the Numbers Are Calculated

The cost-per-year model combines:

  • Depreciation: Modeled as a declining percentage of the used purchase price. The EV9 loses ~7% in year 1 from its used price, tapering to ~3% by year 5+ as it approaches its floor value. The Highlander Hybrid follows a flatter curve (~5% → 2.5%). Residual floor values are estimated at $18k (EV9 uses a larger, newer battery pack with higher eventual replacement risk) and $19.5k (Highlander, reflecting Toyota's stronger long-term resale reputation).
  • Operating costs: Fixed annual amounts of $1,540 (EV9) and $2,170 (HH), assuming level fuel/energy and insurance costs in real terms.
  • Maintenance: EV9 benefits from no oil changes, fewer brake replacements (regen braking), and no transmission service. Highlander Hybrid still requires regular ICE maintenance.

Breakeven Analysis: When Does the EV9's Lower Operating Cost Offset Its Higher Upfront Price?

If you need to stretch the budget to buy an EV9 (purchase cost ~$28,500 for a 2–3 yr old example vs. ~$25,100 for a 2020 Highlander Hybrid), the breakeven question becomes: how many years of lower operating costs does it take to recoup the $3,400 price premium?

Breakeven result: At $0.15/kWh electricity and $4.00/gal gasoline, the EV9's operating cost advantage ($630/yr in fuel + $220/yr in maintenance = $850/yr) recoups the $3,400 premium in about 4 years. If gas prices rise to $5/gal, the gap widens to ~$1,260/yr, pulling breakeven forward to roughly 2.7 years.

Best Time to Buy (Financial Perspective)

Kia EV9

Given the steep early‑year drop, the optimal window for a used EV9 under $25k is likely late 2026 to early 2027, when 2023‑2024 models reach 2‑3 years of age and mileage approaches 30k‑50k. However, even then, finding one under $25k with <75k miles in Southern California may require expanding the search nationally or considering slightly higher mileage.

Breakeven recommendation: If you can stretch the budget to ~$28k–$29k for a 2-year-old EV9, the 4-year breakeven means you'll be ahead by year 5 and save $2k+ over years 5–10. If $25k is a hard cap, you'll need to wait until late 2027 or accept higher mileage.

Toyota Highlander Hybrid

Because the Highlander Hybrid depreciates slowly, the best financial sense lies in purchasing a slightly older model (2018‑2020) with moderate mileage (60k‑80k) that has already absorbed the bulk of early depreciation. For a sub‑$25k target, aim for late 2026 onward, when 2018‑2020 models fall into the price range. Purchasing a CPO example adds a premium but can reduce risk.

Recommendation

Under the strict constraints (<$25k, <75k miles, CPO preferred), both vehicles are viable, but the Toyota Highlander Hybrid is more readily available in the used market today and offers proven reliability and lower risk. If you can wait until late 2026‑early 2027 and are willing to search nationally or consider slightly higher mileage, the Kia EV9 may provide a lower total cost of ownership over 5 years thanks to savings on electricity and maintenance.

Decision framework based on holding period:

  • Planning to keep 3-5 years? Either vehicle works. EV9 wins on cost but has thinner used market. Highlander is easier to source now.
  • Planning to keep 7-10 years? The EV9's advantage grows — you'll save ~$500–$600/yr in operating costs. The longer you keep it, the more the buying premium pays off.
  • Concerned about EV battery longevity? The 10-year residual floor accounts for battery degradation risk. If you keep it past the 10-year/100k-mile warranty, set aside ~$3k–$5k for potential battery module replacement.

Look for:

  • Highlander Hybrid: 2018‑2020 LE/XLE, 60k‑80k miles, purchase window Q3 2026 – Q2 2027.
  • EV9: 2023‑2024 models, ideally <50k miles, purchase window late 2026 – early 2027; be prepared to expand search beyond Southern California. If you can stretch budget to $28k–$29k, you'll likely find a much better selection.

If you can relax the budget to <$30k or mileage to ≤90k, many more options open for both vehicles, and the EV9's operating cost advantage becomes even more compelling.
